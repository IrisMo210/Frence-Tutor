French Grammar · Le calendrier

Jours, mois, dates & saisons

Everything you need to talk about time in French — the days, the months, how to write a date, and the four seasons. Note: French never capitalizes days or months.

1

The days of the week — les jours

The French week starts on Monday. All days are masculine. Use le lundi to mean "on Mondays" (every Monday), and just lundi for "on Monday" (this one).

Memory hook

Five days end in -di (from Latin dies, "day") and hide a planet: mardi = Mars, mercredi = Mercury, jeudi = Jupiter, vendredi = Venus. Dimanche ("Lord's day") is the odd one out.

Watch these

juin (June, "zhwan") vs juillet (July, "zhwee-yay") are easy to mix up. août is pronounced "oot" (the first letters are nearly silent). Don't forget the accents: février, décembre.

3

The four seasons — les saisons

All four seasons are masculine. The preposition for "in" is en — except spring, which uniquely takes au.

The one trap

Three seasons use en, but spring is the exception: au printemps, never "en printemps". Remember: spring stands alone.

4

Numbers for dates — 1 to 31

You need these to say a date. For dates, only the 1st uses a special word — le premier. Every other day is just the plain number.

5

Writing & saying the date

The pattern is le + number + month (+ year) — day number first, then the month, the opposite habit can trip up English speakers writing 14 juillet, not "juillet 14".

Key rules

Ask the date: Quelle est la date ?  Answer: C'est le 3 mars. / On est le 3 mars.

The 1st only: le 1er mai (premier). All others are cardinal: le 2 mai, le 14 juillet.

Years are read in full: 2026 = deux mille vingt-six; "in 2026" = en 2026.

7

How to remember it easily

1

Never capitalize days or months

Unlike English, French writes lundi and janvier in lowercase (unless they start a sentence). This alone fixes a very common mistake.

2

Days hide planets

mardi–vendredi map to Mars, Mercury, Jupiter, Venus. Linking each day to its planet makes the middle of the week effortless.

3

Months are almost free

Ten of the twelve are near-identical to English. Just drill the spelling and accents, and the tricky pair juin / juillet.

4

"Spring stands alone"

For seasons, the rule is en for three and au printemps for one. One exception is easy to hold onto.

5

Only the 1st is special

For dates, memorise just le premier. Everything else is the plain counting number — no ordinals needed.
